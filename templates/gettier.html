Module 02 — The JTB Problem

The Gettier Problem Explorer

Edmund Gettier showed in 1963 that justified true belief is not sufficient for knowledge. Explore the cases — and why every proposed fix has struggled.

The Problem

For much of the history of Western philosophy, knowledge was defined as justified true belief (JTB): S knows P iff P is true, S believes P, and S is justified in believing P. This account has roots in Plato's Meno and Theaetetus and was the dominant view by the mid-20th century.

In a short 1963 paper, Edmund Gettier constructed two cases in which a subject has a justified true belief that is intuitively not knowledge. The cases are structurally similar: a subject reasons validly from a justified false belief to a true conclusion. The conclusion is true, but for the wrong reasons.

Gettier's paper launched fifty years of proposals to fix the JTB account. None has achieved consensus. This module walks through the classic cases and tests the major proposed solutions.

Case I · Smith & Jones
The Job and the Coins
Smith has strong evidence that Jones will get the job, and also knows Jones has 10 coins in his pocket. He infers: "The man who will get the job has 10 coins in his pocket." In fact, Smith gets the job — and unbeknownst to him, also has 10 coins in his pocket.
✓ P is true (Smith — the job-getter — has 10 coins)
✓ Smith believes P
✓ Smith is justified (via valid inference)
✗ Intuitively: Smith does not know P
Case II · Smith & Ford
The Car and the Disjunction
Smith has strong evidence that Jones owns a Ford. He infers the disjunction: "Either Jones owns a Ford, or Brown is in Barcelona." The disjunction is true — but because Brown happens to be in Barcelona, not because Jones owns a Ford (Jones doesn't).
✓ P is true (Brown is in Barcelona)
✓ Smith believes P (via justified inference)
✓ Smith is justified in his inference
✗ Intuitively: Smith does not know P

Proposed Solutions — and Their Problems

1.
No False Lemmas (Harman, 1973)
Knowledge requires that the belief not be inferred from any false intermediate step. Diagnoses the Gettier cases correctly — Smith infers from "Jones owns a Ford" or "Jones will get the job," both false.
Partial fix — fails for cases with no explicit false lemma (Nogot variant)
2.
Causal Theory (Goldman, 1967)
Knowledge requires the right kind of causal connection between the fact and the belief. Works for perceptual knowledge; P's being true must causally contribute to S's believing P.
Fails for mathematical/future knowledge where causal connection is unclear
3.
Reliabilism (Goldman, 1979)
A belief constitutes knowledge if it is produced by a reliable cognitive process — one that tends to produce true beliefs. Gettier cases involve unreliable causal chains despite justified conclusions.
Faces the generality problem: how do we individuate the relevant process type?
4.
Tracking Theory (Nozick, 1981)
Knowledge requires that the belief be sensitive and adherent across possible worlds. Gettier cases fail sensitivity — in nearby worlds where the lucky truth-maker is absent, the belief would still be held.
